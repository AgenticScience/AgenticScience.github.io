From AI for Science to Agentic Science: A Survey on Autonomous Scientific Discovery

Abstract

Artificial intelligence (AI) is bringing a major shift to scientific research. Moving beyond the well-known AI for Science approach—where AI is used as a tool for specific tasks—a new paradigm is taking shape: Agentic Science. In this paradigm, AI systems act as independent agents that can reason, plan, run experiments, and refine ideas on their own to reach scientific goals. This survey introduces the first full framework for this emerging field. We begin by defining Agentic Science and outlining the five key abilities of a scientific agent: (i) planning and reasoning, (ii) tool use and integration, (iii) memory mechanism, (iv) collaborating with other agents, and (v) optimization and evolution. We then describe the agentic discovery process as a flexible four-step cycle: observation and hypothesis generation, experimental planning and execution, result analysis, and evolution. Focusing on how these steps interact, we review recent progress in Agentic Science across the four major natural sciences—life sciences, chemistry, materials science, and physics—covering over a dozen subfields and examining hundreds of studies. Finally, we highlight current challenges and future directions, such as validation, dealing with uncertainty, and working alongside humans, and we suggest a path forward. Agentic Science signals a new era where AI moves from being a tool to becoming a creative partner, speeding up and reshaping how science is done.
